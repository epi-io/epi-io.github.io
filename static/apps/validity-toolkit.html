Methodology, references & how to cite

Content & Face Validity Index (I-CVI / I-FVI)

Each rater scores every item 1–4 (1 = not relevant/clear, 2 = somewhat, 3 = quite, 4 = highly). Ratings of 3 or 4 count as agreement.

I-CVI = (raters rating 3 or 4) / (total raters who rated the item)

S-CVI/Ave — mean of all I-CVIs. Acceptable at ≥0.90 (Polit & Beck, 2006; Polit, Beck & Owen, 2007).
S-CVI/UA — proportion of items with I-CVI = 1.00 (every rater agreed). Acceptable at ≥0.80.

FVI uses identical formulas applied to ratings of item clarity and comprehensibility rather than relevance, typically from a participant panel rather than a subject-matter expert panel.

Modified kappa (chance-corrected agreement)

Raw I-CVI doesn't adjust for the chance that raters agree by luck alone. The modified kappa corrects for this:

Pc = [N! / (A!(N−A)!)] × 0.5^N  —  probability of chance agreement
κ = (I-CVI − Pc) / (1 − Pc)

Interpretation (Cicchetti & Sparrow, 1981; Fleiss, 1981): κ > 0.74 excellent · 0.60–0.74 good · 0.40–0.59 fair · <0.40 poor.

Content Validity Ratio (CVR)

Lawshe's (1975) method asks each expert to rate every item as Essential, Useful but not essential, or Not necessary.

CVR = (ne − N/2) / (N/2), where ne = experts rating the item Essential, N = total experts rating that item. CVR ranges from −1 to +1.

An item is retained if its CVR meets or exceeds the critical value for that panel size. Rather than use Lawshe's original table — later shown to contain calculation errors for larger panels — this tool computes the critical value directly from the exact binomial distribution (one-tailed, α = .05), following Ayre & Scally (2014).

The scale-level mean CVR is the average CVR of items meeting the critical value (Lawshe's original "CVI"); share meeting criterion is the proportion of all items that clear the critical value.
